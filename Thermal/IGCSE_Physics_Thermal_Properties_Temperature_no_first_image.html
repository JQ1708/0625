Thermal Expansion

What happens when heated?

(at constant pressure)

  • Temperature increases
  • Overall volume increases (expands)
  • Density decreases

Why does it expand?

Molecules gain kinetic energy, move/vibrate faster, collide more, and push each other apart.

[Please refer to the diagram of thermal expansion.]

Expansion in Different States

Gases > Liquids > Solids

Solids

Expand the least. Strong forces between particles restrict movement.

Liquids

Expand more than solids. Weaker forces allow more movement.

Gases

Expand the most. Almost no forces between particles, so they are free to move far apart.

Made of two different metals (e.g., steel and brass) bonded together. Since they expand at different rates, the strip bends when heated. This is used in thermostats and fire alarms.

Thermometers üå°Ô∏è

In a liquid-in-glass thermometer, the liquid (mercury or alcohol) expands predictably when heated, causing it to rise up the narrow tube.

Specific Heat Capacity

What is Specific Heat Capacity?

The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 ¬∞C.

The SHC Equation

ΔE = m × c × Δθ

ΔE = change in thermal energy in Joules (J)

m = mass in kilograms (kg)

c = specific heat capacity in J / kg ¬∞C

Δθ = change in temperature in degrees Celsius (¬∞C)

This equation can be rearranged to find specific heat capacity, c:
c = ΔE ÷ (m × Δθ)

Substances with HIGH SHC...

  • Require more energy to heat up.
  • Store large amounts of energy.
  • Heat up and cool down slowly.
  • Example: Water (used in central heating systems).

Substances with LOW SHC...

  • Require less energy to heat up.
  • Are good thermal conductors.
  • Heat up and cool down quickly.
  • Example: Metals (used for cooking pans).

Step 1: List knowns & rearrange equation

  • Change in energy, ΔE = 3800 J
  • Mass, m = 2 kg
  • Change in temperature, Δθ = 5 ¬∞C

c = ΔE ÷ (m × Δθ)

Step 2: Substitute values

c = 3800 ÷ (2 × 5)

c = 3800 ÷ 10

c = 380 J / kg ¬∞C

Investigating Specific Heat Capacity

[Please refer to the diagrams for setting up the experiment for a solid block and a liquid.]

Procedure:

  1. Measure the mass (m) of the substance (e.g., a metal block) using a balance. For a liquid, measure the mass of the empty container first, then the container with the liquid.
  2. Place a thermometer and an immersion heater into the substance.
  3. Measure the initial temperature (θ1).
  4. Connect the heater to a joulemeter and a power supply.
  5. Turn on the power supply and start a stopwatch.
  6. After some time (e.g., 10 minutes), switch off the power.
  7. Record the final energy reading (E) from the joulemeter. (If a joulemeter is not available, use E = V × I × t by measuring voltage, current, and time).
  8. Record the highest final temperature (θ2) reached.

Improving Accuracy:

The biggest source of error is heat loss to the surroundings. This can be reduced by wrapping the block or container in an insulating material (like cotton wool) and putting a lid on for liquids.

Melting & Boiling

Heating Curve Explained

[Please refer to the diagram of a heating/cooling curve.]

Sloped Sections

Energy supplied increases the kinetic energy of particles, so the temperature rises. The substance is in a single state (solid, liquid, or gas).

Flat Sections (Plateaus)

Energy supplied is latent heat. It's used to break intermolecular bonds and increase particle potential energy. The temperature stays constant while the substance changes state.

Cooling Curve

A cooling curve is the reverse. Latent heat is released as bonds form during condensing and freezing, so the temperature stays constant at these points.

Specific Latent Heat

What is Specific Latent Heat (L)?

The amount of energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without any change in temperature.

The Latent Heat Equation

ΔE = m × L

ΔE = thermal energy for a change of state in Joules (J)

m = mass in kilograms (kg)

L = specific latent heat in J / kg

Specific Latent Heat of Fusion (Lf)

Energy to change 1 kg of a substance from solid to liquid at its melting point.

Specific Latent Heat of Vaporisation (Lv)

Energy to change 1 kg of a substance from liquid to gas at its boiling point.

Evaporation

What is Evaporation?

The escape of the most energetic molecules from the surface of a liquid. This can happen at any temperature.

The Cooling Effect

Since the most energetic ("hottest") particles leave, the average kinetic energy of the remaining particles decreases. This means the liquid cools down. This is used in refrigerators and air conditioners.

Factors Affecting Rate of Evaporation

Temperature üå°Ô∏è

Higher temperature means particles have more kinetic energy, so more of them can escape. Rate increases.

Surface Area üíß

A larger surface area means more molecules are at the surface, ready to escape. Rate increases.

Air Movement üå¨Ô∏è

Wind or a fan blows away evaporated particles from above the surface, allowing more to escape. Rate increases.

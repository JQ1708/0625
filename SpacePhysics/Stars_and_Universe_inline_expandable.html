1. The Sun as a Star

Our Star: The Sun

  • The Sun is a medium-sized star at the centre of our Solar System.
  • It consists mostly of hydrogen and helium.
  • It radiates most of its energy in the infrared, visible, and ultraviolet parts of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Nuclear Fusion in Stars (Extended Tier Only)

  • Stars are giant balls of (mostly) hydrogen gas.
  • In a star's core, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium nuclei, releasing a huge amount of energy.
  • A possible fusion reaction involves two hydrogen isotopes, Deuterium (21H) and Tritium (31H), which are formed through other fusion reactions in the star.

All stable stars are powered by these nuclear fusion reactions, which release a huge amount of energy.

2. The Scale of the Universe

Our Cosmic Address

The Universe contains many structures, ordered by size:

  1. The Solar System: The Sun and all the objects that orbit it (planets, moons, asteroids).
  2. The Milky Way Galaxy: A collection of billions of stars, including our Solar System, held together by gravity.
  3. The Universe: A vast space containing billions of galaxies.

Orbital Motion

  • An orbit is the path a celestial body (like a planet or moon) takes around another.
  • This motion is caused by gravitational attraction, which provides the force that keeps the object on its path. This force is always directed towards the centre of the larger body.
  • For a stable orbit, if the object's speed increases, its orbital radius must also increase. (Extended Tier Only)

Measuring Space: The Light-Year

  • A light-year is the distance light travels through a vacuum in one year.
  • It is a unit of distance, not time.
  • Since light travels incredibly fast, this unit is used for the enormous distances between stars and galaxies.

3. The Life Cycle of a Star

From Nebula to Star

  1. Nebula: Stars form from giant clouds of dust and gas (mostly hydrogen). Gravitational attraction between particles pulls them together.
  2. Protostar: As the cloud becomes more concentrated, it forms a hot, dense sphere. As more particles are pulled in, its density and temperature increase dramatically.
  3. Main Sequence Star: When the core is hot and dense enough for nuclear fusion to begin, a stable star is born. During this stable period, the outward pressure from thermal expansion is balanced by the inward force of gravity. Our Sun is a main sequence star.

The End of a Star's Life

Eventually, the hydrogen in the star's core runs out, fusion stops, and the core begins to collapse and heat up, starting fusion of helium and other elements. This leads to two main paths...

Path 1: Average Stars (like our Sun)

Red Giant

The star swells, cools, and turns red as it fuses heavier elements.

Planetary Nebula

The star becomes unstable, pulsates, and ejects its outer layers of dust and gas.

White Dwarf

The hot, dense solid core is left behind. It cools over billions of years into a black dwarf.

Path 2: Massive Stars

Red Supergiant

The star swells up, but much larger than a red giant, undergoing collapses and expansions as it fuses heavier elements up to iron.

Supernova

When iron forms, fusion stops. The core collapses, and the star explodes, scattering heavy elements ‚Äî the building blocks of new planets and life ‚Äî across space.

Neutron Star or Black Hole

The core collapses into an extremely dense neutron star, or if massive enough, a black hole with gravity so strong not even light can escape.

4. Cosmic Theories & Evidence

Galactic Redshift

  • Redshift is the observation of electromagnetic radiation (like light) from an object moving away from an observer, causing its wavelength to increase.
  • This is similar to the Doppler effect for sound: as a source moves away, its waves are stretched out. For light, this shifts it towards the red end of the spectrum.
  • The light from almost all galaxies is redshifted.
  • The further away a galaxy is, the greater its redshift.
  • This provides evidence that the Universe is expanding.

The Big Bang Theory

This is the leading scientific theory stating that the Universe began approximately 13.7 billion years ago from an infinitely hot, dense single point which exploded and has been expanding ever since.

Key Evidence:

  1. Galactic Redshift: Shows that galaxies are moving away from us, supporting the idea of a continuous expansion from a single point.
  2. Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) Radiation: This is faint microwave radiation detected from all directions in space. It is believed to be the "afterglow" or leftover heat from the Big Bang.

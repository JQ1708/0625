Background Radiation

What is Background Radiation?

(Tap to reveal)

  • It is defined as the low levels of radiation that exist around us all the time.
  • The level of background radiation can vary significantly from place to place.
  • It's crucial to subtract the background count rate from your measurements in experiments to get the true count rate from the source alone.

Two Types of Sources

  • Natural Sources: From radioactive elements that have always existed on Earth and in space.
  • Man-Made Sources: From human activities like medical procedures and industrial uses.

Main Sources of Background Radiation

Approximate percentage contributions:

  • Radon Gas (50%): From rocks and soil; the largest contributor.
  • Rocks & Buildings (15%): Many building materials contain naturally radioactive substances.
  • Medical (13%): X-rays, CT scans, and radioactive tracers used in medicine.
  • Food & Drink (11%): All living things contain radioactive isotopes like Carbon-14.
  • Cosmic Rays (10%): High-energy particles from space, mainly from the Sun.

Types of Radiation

  • The GM tube gives a "count rate" - the number of radioactive particles detected per second (or minute).
  • Each time a particle of radiation enters the tube, it makes a clicking sound.

Alpha (Œ±)

  • Nature: A Helium nucleus (2p, 2n).
  • Charge: +2
  • Speed: Slowest type.
  • Penetration: Stopped by paper.
  • Ionising Power: Very high.

Beta (Œ≤)

  • Nature: A high-speed electron.
  • Charge: -1
  • Speed: Fast.
  • Penetration: Stopped by ~3mm aluminium.
  • Ionising Power: Medium.

Gamma (Œ≥)

  • Nature: An EM wave.
  • Charge: 0 (neutral).
  • Speed: Speed of light.
  • Penetration: Reduced by thick lead.
  • Ionising Power: Low.

What is Ionisation?

Ionisation is the process where radiation knocks electrons out of atoms, turning them into charged ions. This is what damages living cells.

Deflection in Fields

  • Alpha (Œ±): Deflected, as it is charged (+2). Its large mass means it deflects less than beta.
  • Beta (Œ≤): Deflected in the opposite direction to alpha, as it is charged (-1). Its tiny mass means it deflects much more than alpha.
  • Gamma (Œ≥): Not deflected at all, as it has no charge.

Radioactive Decay

What is Radioactive Decay?

(Tap to reveal)

  • The process where an unstable atomic nucleus gives out radiation to become more stable.
  • It is a random and spontaneous process. It is impossible to predict which nucleus will decay next.
  • When a nucleus decays, its structure (number of protons/neutrons) can change, forming a different element.

Nuclide Notation

A X Z
  • X = Element Symbol
  • A (Mass Number): Total protons + neutrons.
  • Z (Atomic Number): Number of protons.

Gamma (Œ≥) Decay

The nucleus emits a gamma ray to lose excess energy. It does not change the element.

  • Mass number (A) and Atomic number (Z) do not change.

Half-Life

What is Half-Life?

(Tap to reveal)

  • The time it takes for the number of unstable nuclei in a sample to halve.
  • Alternatively, the time it takes for the count rate from a sample to fall to half its initial level.
  • The half-life for a particular isotope is constant and is unaffected by external factors like temperature and pressure.

Using a Decay Curve

(Tap to reveal)

  • Find the half-life from a graph of count rate vs. time.
  • 1. Read the initial count rate (at time = 0).
  • 2. Divide this value by 2.
  • 3. Find this halved count rate on the y-axis and trace across to the curve, then down to the x-axis to read the half-life.

Uses of Radiation

Alpha (Œ±) - Smoke Detectors

  • Americium-241 is used. Its alpha particles ionise air, creating a current.
  • Smoke absorbs the alpha particles, the current drops, and an alarm sounds.

Beta (Œ≤) - Thickness Control

  • Used for materials like paper or aluminium foil. A detector on the other side measures the count rate.
  • If the material is too thick, the count rate drops, and rollers press harder (and vice-versa).

Gamma (Œ≥) - Medical Uses

  • Sterilisation: Kills bacteria on medical equipment.
  • Radiotherapy: A focused beam kills cancerous tumours.
  • Tracers: A beta or gamma emitter with a short half-life is injected. Its movement is tracked to check organ function. Alpha is not used as it's stopped by the body.

Gamma (Œ≥) - Industrial Uses

  • Checking Welds: A gamma source is placed on one side of a welded joint and photographic film on the other. Weak points or cracks show up as darker areas on the film.
  • Finding Leaks: A gamma emitter is added to an oil or gas pipeline. A detector is run along the outside to find areas of high radioactivity, indicating a leak.

Dangers & Safety

The Dangers of Ionising Radiation

Ionising radiation can damage living cells:

  • It can cause mutations in DNA, potentially leading to cancer.
  • Very high doses kill cells, causing acute radiation poisoning (symptoms include hair loss and vomiting).

Contamination vs. Irradiation

  • Irradiation is exposure from an external source. The danger stops when the source is removed.
    • Beta (Œ≤) and Gamma (Œ≥) are the biggest risks here, as they can penetrate the skin.
  • Contamination is when a radioactive source gets inside the body. The danger continues.
    • Alpha (Œ±) is the most dangerous contaminant due to its high ionising power at short range.

Radioactive Waste

  • Waste from nuclear reactors often has a very long half-life.
  • High-level waste is stored deep underground in sealed containers to prevent environmental contamination.

Safety Precautions

To minimise exposure, always:

  • Limit Exposure Time
  • Increase Distance (use tongs)
  • Use Shielding (lead, concrete)
  • Store sources in a lead-lined container.

Current in Circuits

Electric Current

  • Electric current is the rate of flow of charge.
  • It is measured in amperes (A) using an ammeter.
  • Ammeters must be connected in series.
  • Conventional current flows from positive to negative.

Current in Series & Parallel

  • Series Circuit: Current is the same at all points. (Itotal = I1 = I2 = ...)
  • Parallel Circuit: Current is split between the branches. The total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it. (Itotal = I1 + I2 + ...)

E.M.F & Potential Difference

Definitions

  • Potential Difference (p.d.): Also known as voltage, it is the work done per unit charge. It's the energy transferred *by* the charge to a component. Measured in volts (V).
  • Electromotive Force (e.m.f): The work done per unit charge *by the source* (e.g., a battery). It is the total energy supplied to the circuit. Also measured in volts (V).
  • A voltmeter is used to measure p.d. and e.m.f, and must be connected in parallel across a component.

Voltage in Series & Parallel

  • Series Circuit: The total e.m.f. from the source is shared between the components. (Vsource = V1 + V2 + ...)
  • Parallel Circuit: The potential difference is the same across all branches. (Vsource = V1 = V2 = ...)

Ohm's Law & Resistance

  • Resistance (R): The opposition to the flow of current. A higher resistance means it's harder for current to flow. Measured in ohms (Œ©).
  • Ohm's Law: For a conductor at constant temperature, the current is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.

Potential Dividers

How They Work

  • A potential divider circuit consists of two or more resistors in series.
  • The input voltage (e.m.f.) is shared between the resistors in proportion to their resistance.
  • This allows you to get a desired fraction of the source voltage as an output.

Electrical Safety

Wiring a Plug

  • Live Wire (Brown): Carries the high potential. Dangerous.
  • Neutral Wire (Blue): Completes the circuit. Usually at 0V.
  • Earth Wire (Green/Yellow Stripes): Safety wire. Connects the metal casing to the ground. It carries current away safely if a fault causes the live wire to touch the casing.

Fuses & Trip Switches

  • A fuse is a thin piece of wire designed to melt and break the circuit if the current gets too high. It must be placed on the live wire.
  • You must choose a fuse with a rating just above the normal operating current of the appliance. Common ratings are 3A, 5A, and 13A.
  • A trip switch (or circuit breaker) is an automatic electromagnetic switch that breaks the circuit if the current is too high. They are resettable and react faster than fuses.

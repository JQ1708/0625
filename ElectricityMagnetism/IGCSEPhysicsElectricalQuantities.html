Electric Charge & Fields

Objects become charged by gaining (negative charge) or losing (positive charge) electrons. Like charges repel, opposite charges attract. An electric field is a region where a charge experiences a force. Field lines show the direction of the force on a positive charge.

Conductors & Insulators

Conductors (like metals) are materials that allow charge to flow through them easily because they have free electrons. Insulators (like plastic or rubber) do not have free electrons and do not allow charge to flow.

Insulators can be charged by friction. For example, rubbing a plastic rod with a cloth can transfer electrons from one to the other, leaving both objects with an equal and opposite charge.

Current, E.M.F & P.D.

Current (I) is the rate of flow of charge. Conventional current flows from + to -.

I = Qt

E.M.F (Œµ) is the energy supplied per unit charge by a source. P.D. (V) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points.

V = WQ

Measuring Current & Voltage

An ammeter measures current and must be connected in series. A voltmeter measures potential difference and must be connected in parallel.

Resistance & Component Characteristics

Resistance (R) is the opposition to current.

R = VI

Resistance of a wire increases with length and decreases with cross-sectional area.

Electrical Energy & Power

Power (P) is the rate at which energy is transferred, measured in Watts (W).

P = V × I

P = I²R

P = V²/R

Electrical energy is measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh). 1 kWh is the energy used by a 1 kW device for 1 hour.
